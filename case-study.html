01 — Context & Challenge

A hospital network growing faster than its scheduling could handle

As Reina Madre / María Linda expanded across specialties, scheduling became one of its most critical operational pain points. Each department maintained its own process — some used spreadsheets, others used phone calls, and a few relied on whiteboards. There was no shared visibility, no unified logic, and no reliable way to prevent conflicts.

The consequences were real: double-booked appointments, missed resource availability, wasted surgical time, and frustrated clinical staff dealing with escalations that should never have happened.

I was brought in to redesign the platform from the ground up — not just the UI, but the underlying scheduling logic, information architecture, and the workflows that clinical and administrative staff would use every day.

The core problem
"We had 12 departments managing scheduling independently. A patient needing consultation across two specialties required manual coordination between two separate systems — or two phone calls."
— Clinical Operations Director (paraphrased for confidentiality)

02 — Research

Embedded in the clinical environment

Before touching a wireframe, I spent two weeks conducting contextual inquiry with the primary users: scheduling coordinators, clinic receptionists, department heads, and physicians who interacted with the scheduling system indirectly.

Research methods used

  • Observation sessions with scheduling coordinators in their daily context
  • Semi-structured interviews with department heads and clinical leads
  • Shadowing receptionists during peak scheduling periods
  • Workflow mapping workshops with operations management
  • Analysis of scheduling conflict logs from the previous 6 months

Key findings

Finding 1 — Mental models differ by role
Coordinators think in terms of time blocks and resource availability. Physicians think in terms of patient conditions and appointment type. The system needed to support both mental models simultaneously.
Finding 2 — Conflict detection was manual
100% of double-booking incidents required manual discovery — either a patient called back to confirm, or a coordinator checked the physical board. No system-level conflict detection existed.

Information architecture

The biggest structural challenge was creating a hierarchy that made sense across different department types — some scheduling by resource (room, equipment), others by provider (physician, nurse), and others by patient type. I ran card-sorting sessions with coordinators to understand how they naturally categorized appointment types.

Key design decisions

  • Single scheduling view with department filters — instead of separate systems per department
  • Conflict detection surface at booking time — not after the fact
  • Resource availability as a first-class element — rooms, equipment, and providers visible in context
  • Role-based views — coordinators see resource grid; physicians see patient queue; admins see reports

04 — Solution

A unified scheduling layer for all departments

The final system brought all scheduling activity into a single, role-aware interface. The core scheduling grid provided real-time visibility into availability across all departments — with conflict detection that surfaced issues before they became problems, not after.

Core features shipped

  • Real-time scheduling grid with multi-department overlay
  • Automated conflict detection with inline resolution suggestions
  • Resource management panel (rooms, equipment, providers)
  • Appointment type system with custom scheduling rules per specialty
  • Role-based access and view modes
  • Basic analytics dashboard for department managers

06 — Learnings

What this project taught me

Healthcare scheduling is deceptively complex. On the surface it looks like a calendar. Underneath it's a multi-constraint optimization problem involving time, physical space, human availability, regulatory requirements, and patient urgency — and any design that ignores that complexity will break in production.

  • Invest in workflow mapping before wireframing — the discovery phase saved weeks of rework
  • Role-based design is not optional in multi-stakeholder clinical systems
  • Conflict detection UX is about the language, not just the indicator — how you phrase a conflict determines whether staff trust the system
  • Adoption requires change management support alongside the product launch — I worked with operations to create onboarding materials
