About

I solve operational problems
through design.

8+ years designing complex healthcare systems, enterprise platforms, and digital products that affect real clinical decisions.

I started as a graphic designer, but I was always more interested in how things work than how they look. That instinct led me into UX — and eventually into one of the most demanding environments a designer can operate in: healthcare.

For the past several years, I've been designing clinical systems at Reina Madre / María Linda — a hospital network where the products I design are used by doctors performing procedures, nurses managing inpatient beds, and administrators coordinating care across departments. There is no "ideal user" here. There's a surgeon who has 90 seconds between patients. There's a coordinator juggling 14 simultaneous admissions. The design has to work for all of them.

My process starts with understanding the operational reality before touching a frame. That means embedded observation with clinical staff, requirements sessions with stakeholders, flow diagrams that map every state and exception, and validation testing before anything ships. I don't design in isolation — I work directly with developers, clinical leads, and operations teams to make sure the product I deliver is the product that gets used.

Before healthcare, I led UX projects across transportation, education, restaurants, and professional services at a marketing agency — which gave me the discipline of working fast across unfamiliar domains. Before that, I was at a professional football association, doing web design and digital communication.

What unifies all of it: I care about systems that function under real conditions, not just systems that look good in a prototype.

How I think about design

Understand the system before designing the screen Every interface exists inside an operational context. I map that context before I open a frame.
Edge cases are not optional In healthcare, what breaks in edge cases can hurt people. I design for exceptions, not just happy paths.
Research is a design tool, not a phase I talk to users continuously — before, during, and after design — not just at the start of a sprint.
Stakeholder buy-in is part of the design outcome A design that doesn't get adopted isn't a good design. I treat organizational alignment as part of the problem.
Simplicity is earned, not assumed The most minimal interface is often the hardest to create — it requires understanding what can be hidden, not what can be removed.
